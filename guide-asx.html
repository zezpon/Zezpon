ASX basics

Start with the essentials of the Australian share market, from sectors and indices to the language used in daily market coverage.

Overview

The Australian Securities Exchange (ASX) is where investors buy and sell shares in publicly listed Australian companies. It also includes exchange-traded funds (ETFs), investment products, and other securities.

For beginners, the ASX is often the first step into investing because it provides access to familiar businesses and a regulated market environment.

Why it matters

Understanding how the ASX works helps investors make more informed decisions. Before buying any share, it is important to know what you are actually purchasing, how prices move, and what risks are involved.

Key concepts

  • Shares: part ownership in a company. If you own shares, you own a small piece of that business.
  • Market price: the current price buyers and sellers agree on in the market. This price changes throughout the trading day.
  • Ticker code: the short code used to identify a company or fund on the exchange. Every listed company has its own code.
  • Dividends: payments some companies make to shareholders from profits.
  • Capital growth: the increase in the value of an investment over time.

How buying shares works

When you buy a share, you are not buying from the company directly in most cases. You are usually buying from another investor who is willing to sell. The market matches buyers and sellers based on price.

To invest, most people use a brokerage platform. Once an order is placed, it is either filled at the available market price or only at a specific price if a limit order is used.

What beginners should focus on first

Start by learning the difference between a strong business and a popular stock. A company can be well known, but that does not always make it a good investment.

Beginners should also understand that share prices move for many reasons, including company performance, market sentiment, industry trends, interest rates, and broader economic conditions.

Common beginner mistakes

One common mistake is buying a stock only because it has recently gone up. Another is concentrating too much money into one company or one theme. Beginners also often confuse a good company with a good buying opportunity at any price.

Simple checklist before investing

  • What does the company actually do?
  • How does it make money?
  • Is the business profitable or growing sustainably?
  • What risks could affect it?
  • Am I investing, or am I just reacting to hype?

Quick takeaway

The ASX is a place where ownership in businesses is bought and sold. A beginner does not need to know everything at once, but they do need to understand what they own, why they own it, and what risks they are taking.
